1.1 Introduction to Quantum Superposition

Quantum mechanics is a fundamental theory in physics that provides a description of the physical properties of nature at the scale of atoms and subatomic particles. It is the foundation of all quantum physics including quantum chemistry, quantum field theory, quantum technology, and quantum information science.

The superposition principle states that any two (or more) quantum states can be added together ("superposed") and the result will be another valid quantum state; and conversely, that every quantum state can be represented as a sum of two or more other distinct states.

Key Concept: Schrödinger's Cat

A thought experiment that illustrates the paradox of superposition in the macroscopic world, where a cat can be considered both alive and dead until observed.

In mathematical terms, if |ψ1⟩ and |ψ2⟩ are two states of a quantum system, then c1|ψ1⟩ + c2|ψ2⟩ is also a state of the system for any complex numbers c1 and c2. This is fundamentally different from classical physics where particles occupy specific points in space-time.
